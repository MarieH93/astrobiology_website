Geological features of Mars

Mars has some of the largest geological features in our solar system.
Its ancient environments may have haboured life 3.8 billion years ago.

Martian Geology
Olympus Mons
Valles Marineris

Like Earth, Mars is a differentiated planet, meaning that it has a central core made up of metallic iron and nickel surrounded by a less dense, silicate mantle and crust. Mars appears as a red planet due to the iron-oxide. Scientists think that the most abundant chemical elements in the Martian crust are iron, magnesium, aluminium, calcium, and potassium. These elements are major components of the minerals comprising igneous rocks.

Olympus Mons is the largest volcano in the Solar System and is three times as tall as Mount Everest. The reason for why Olympus Mons and the other volcanoes on Mars are large is because its crust doesn't move like Earth’s. Lack of active plate tectonic means that rising magma erupted on the same part of the planet's crust and slowly built up a very large volcano.

The largest canyon in the Solar System -The Mariner Valley. The valley is over 3,000 km long, spans as much as 600 km across, and is 8 km deep. By comparison, the Earth's Grand Canyon in Arizona, USA is 800 km long, 30 km across, and 1.8 km deep. It has been recently suggested that Valles Marineris is a large Tectonic crack in the crust. Most researchers agree that this formed as the crust thickened near Olympus Mons and was widened by erosion.
